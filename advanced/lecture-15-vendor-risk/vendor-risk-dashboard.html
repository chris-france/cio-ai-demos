The CIO Lesson

Your negotiating power is directly proportional to your ability to leave. Salesforce knows you can't leave — so they have all the leverage. Snowflake knows you can leave — so they compete on value. Before signing any AI vendor contract, answer this: "How hard is it to leave in 12 months?" If the answer is "very hard," negotiate harder on pricing caps and exit terms now.

Contract Clause Recommendations

Essential clauses to negotiate into every AI vendor contract. Print this page and hand it to your legal team.

1

Data Portability

Required Clause:

"Vendor shall provide complete export of all customer data, including configurations, customizations, workflow definitions, and historical logs, in industry-standard open formats (JSON, CSV, Parquet, or SQL dump) within 30 days of written request, at no additional charge."

Why it matters: Without this, vendors can hold your data hostage. Salesforce's CSV export loses relationships between objects. Make sure "all data" includes metadata, audit logs, and AI training data you contributed.

Red flag:

"Data export available via API" — this means rate-limited, partial, and you're doing all the work. Reject this.

2

Pricing Caps

Required Clause:

"Annual price increases shall not exceed 5% or CPI, whichever is lower, for the duration of the agreement including renewals. Usage-based pricing shall include a monthly spend cap with 30-day advance notice before any overage charges."

Why it matters: AI vendors are still figuring out pricing. OpenAI has changed pricing 4+ times. Without caps, your budget is at their mercy. Usage-based (Snowflake) is especially dangerous without guardrails.

Red flag:

"Pricing subject to change with 30 days notice" — this is a blank check. Always negotiate firm caps.

3

Exit Terms

Required Clause:

"Either party may terminate with 90 days written notice. Upon termination, vendor shall provide a 6-month transition period at current pricing, during which full data export, API access, and technical support shall remain available. No early termination fees."

Why it matters: Most vendors auto-renew and require 120-180 days notice. Miss the window, you're locked for another year. The transition period is critical — you need time to migrate, not a cliff edge.

Red flag:

"Early termination fee of remaining contract value" — this makes leaving economically impossible. Never accept.

4

SLA Requirements

Required Clause:

"Vendor guarantees 99.9% uptime measured monthly. Each 0.1% below SLA triggers a 10% credit on monthly charges. Three consecutive months below 99.5% shall constitute a material breach, entitling customer to terminate without penalty."

Why it matters: AI services have outages more frequently than traditional SaaS. OpenAI has had multiple multi-hour outages. Credits with teeth force vendors to invest in reliability.

Red flag:

"Best efforts uptime" or SLA credits capped at 5% of monthly fee — this means the SLA is decorative, not contractual.

5

AI Model Stability

Required Clause (AI-Specific):

"Vendor shall maintain access to the specific model version in use at contract signing for a minimum of 12 months. Any model deprecation shall include 6 months advance notice, parallel availability of old and new models, and documented migration guide."

Why it matters: AI vendors regularly deprecate models (GPT-3.5 → GPT-4, etc.). If your workflows depend on specific model behavior, a surprise deprecation can break production systems. This is unique to AI contracts.

6

Data Usage & Training

Required Clause (AI-Specific):

"Vendor shall NOT use customer data, prompts, outputs, or any derivative thereof, for training, fine-tuning, or improving vendor models without explicit written consent. Customer retains all rights to outputs generated using their data."

Why it matters: Many AI vendors train on your data by default. This means your proprietary information could appear in competitors' outputs. Enterprise agreements typically opt out, but verify — and get it in writing.

Rule 1: No Single Points of Failure

Every critical capability must have a tested secondary vendor. Not just identified — tested. Run a quarterly "fire drill" where you process real workloads through the backup vendor.

Rule 2: API Abstraction Layer

Build an internal abstraction layer between your apps and AI vendor APIs. One interface, multiple backends. Switching vendors becomes a config change, not a rewrite. Cost: ~2 weeks of engineering. Value: priceless in a crisis.

Rule 3: Annual Exit Rehearsal

Once a year, actually export all data from each vendor and verify it's usable. Many "export" features produce incomplete or unusable data. Better to discover this during a drill than during a crisis.
